## Start by removing ambiguity

High-trust interfaces rarely begin by adding more. They usually start by removing uncertainty: unclear hierarchy, competing accents, vague labels, and over-explained components.

### Make structure visible

A page should explain itself before the user reads every sentence. Layout, spacing, and rhythm do a surprising amount of product work.

#### Questions I ask while reviewing a screen

- Is the primary action obvious in under three seconds?
- Does every card earn its border, shadow, and metadata?
- Would this still make sense with motion turned off?

## Build a shared visual language

Design systems are most useful when they create alignment without flattening every page into the same composition.

### Tokens create leverage

A restrained token layer makes it easier to tune a product without rewriting entire components.

### Components should teach consistency

Reusable components are not just implementation shortcuts. They are a compact way to encode judgment about hierarchy, spacing, interaction, and fallback behavior.

## Editorial discipline matters in product UI too

Even highly technical products benefit from editorial thinking. Good headings, clear transitions, and purposeful spacing help users understand where they are and what comes next.

### Good UI feels calm because the team made many small decisions on purpose

That calm is usually the result of strong defaults, careful copy, and a willingness to say no to visual clutter.
